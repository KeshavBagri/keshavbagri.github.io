TeamKART: Official FSAE Team of IIT KGP

Website

TeamKART is a group of students from Indian Institute of Technology Kharagpur who like to explore the practical side of automotive engineering. TeamKART is officially a sponsored research project under the Sponsored Research and Industrial Consultancy Cell (SRIC) and the Department of Mechanical Engineering, IIT Kharagpur.

I contributed to the project K5 which has been one of the most successful endeavours of the team so far. As a member of the Powertrain subsystem, I collaborated in the design of the following components for the vehicle:

  • 3D printed Intake manifold
    FSAE rules dictate that the intake system should consist of a restrictor of 20 mm dia. in order to limit power output of the engine. Due to the presence of a restrictor, a significant pressure drop is generated and this drop is recovered with a diffuser, after which the mass of air enters into the plenum, which serves as a reservoir of air, almost at atmospheric pressure. Following the plenum, the last component is the runner whose length is optimised to generate max engine power at a given RPM. We use KTM DUKE 390 which is a 4 stroke single cylinder engine. The entire design is initially simulated on Ricardo WAVE and based on the results obtained, the steady-state Ansys Fluent Analysis is performed. This process is iterated until an acceptable engine performance and fluid behavior is obtained.
  • Fuel Tank
    Our fuel tank is made using industrial grade Aluminium. This ensures significant weight reduction compared to using steel. The key feature of the design is to ensure minimum pressure drop on the fuel pump so as to prevent any disturbance to the engine's fuel supply. We introduce baffles to serve this purpose. Lastly, the mounting of the fuel tank should be such that it can sustain the longitudinal and lateral accelerations that the vehicle can achieve, keeping the flexibility in mind. The total capacity of the fuel tank to sustain the Endurance Event in FSAE competitions is estimated by simulations using Optimum Lap developed by OptimumG.
  • Drivetrain arrangement
    The drivetrain package is composed of a chain-sprocket drive, Drexler Limited Slip Differential and driveshafts for the rear wheels. We use Optimum Lap developed by OptimumG as a simulation tool to calculate the optimum Final Drive Ratio (FDR) to achieve the desired lap times in the dynamic events in FSAE competitions, using the engine and vehicle's parameters. After calculating the FDR, we design the sprocket and perform steady-state static and fatigue analysis on the same to ensure its reliabilty. The driveshafts are designed considering the required lengths, diameters of the wheel hubs and CV joints. It is ensured that the 2 driveshafts have equal torsional stiffness.
  • Downflow Radiator
    We designed the radiator considering the heat required to be dissipated and packaging constraints. Our primary goal was to calculate the required convective area satisfying the constraints. Using the LMTD method to perform the calculations, we obtained the required dimensions and then got it manufactured. The airflow was enhanced using 2 axial fans selected to ensure the required mass flow rate of air for efficient heat rejection.

With respect to the team's Electric vehicle design, I contributed to the following:

  • Motor selection and transmission system
    We shortlisted a variety of motors based on cost, availablity in India, controllability, etc. Following this, we performed multiple simulations on Optimum Lap to select the motor to achieve the required lap time. Planetary gear arrangement coupled with chain-sprocket drive was the transmission system finalized to satisfy the torque requirements of the vehicle. Steady-state static and fatigue analysis were performed to finalize the design and material selection.
  • Cooling system
    The selected motor was air-cooled by design. We performed thermal analysis to ensure its cooling performance. The motor controller was cooled by forced convection using heat sinks and axial fans. All the simulations were performed on Ansys ICEPAK.

For our current project K6, we are introducing multiple design changes such as aerodynamic package, 10" wheels, telemetry system compared to previous vehicles in order to enhance the vehicle's performance in the dynamic events. One of such enhancements is the reactive muffler. Based on our literature review, we finalised 2 variants considering the required insertion loss of 25 dB, minimum backpressure and weight. The fluid analysis was performed on the Ansys Fluent to estimate the backpressure and understand the fluid motion. Acoustic simulations on COMSOL Multiphysics to estimate the transmission loss and compare the designs.

References

  • Design and fabrication of intake manifold for formula SAE (Society of Automotive Engineers) race car by Sylvie Doré, Patrice Lavallée
  • FSAE Electric Vehicle Cooling System Design by Jeff LaMarre
  • Design principles for an automotive muffler by Vinod Sherekar and P. R. Dhamangaonkar
  • Design and fabrication of reactive muffler by G. Gnanendhar Reddy and N. Prakash

Mathematical modeling of Li-ion batteries with focus on Silicon anode particles

Supervisor: Dr. Jeevanjyoti Chakraborty, Mechanical Engineering Department, IIT KGP

A crystalline silicon anode has a theoretical specific capacity of 3600 mAh/g, approximately ten times that of commonly used graphite anodes. This makes it a suitable candidate to be used as an electrode in Li-ion batteries in order to maximise battery's capacity and minimize its weight simultaneously. However, during the charging and discharging of the Lithium Silicon battery, the lattice distance between silicon atoms multiplies as it accommodates lithium ions (lithiation), reaching 320% of the original volume. Due to this massive expansion, stresses of large magnitudes are generated which ultimately leads to the fracture of the silicon material.

This project is aimed at understanding the behaviour of Silicon in these batteries, identify the stress generation and propose a solution to this problem in the long run. The mathematical modelling approach was selected because of the involvement of Partial Differential Equations (PDE) and numerical analysis. In the first stage of the project, I studied the stress generation and diffusion of Li+ ions during potentiostatic (constant voltage operation) and galvanostatic (constant current operation) by solving the PDEs with appropriate boundary conditions. The stress due to diffusion (lithiation) was considered.

The next stage of the project was to formulate a bidirectional relationship between stress and Li+ ion concentration over time. This was considered to be crucial because of the fact that as time progresses, stress increases and hence can have an effect on the concentration which in turn would affect the cell chemistry. The concept of chemical potential was used to establish a connection between the stress and concentration. I solved the involved PDEs using Python and modelled them using COMSOL Multiphysics for visualisation.

In the final phase of my contribution, we moved on to identify crack development behaviour in the Silicon material due to the generated stresses. The amorphous lithiated silicon formed around the crystalline Silicon anode has different regions with elastic and plastic properties. Due to this, the mechanical equilibrium and diffusion equations have different boundary conditions.

References

  • Evolution of stress within a spherical insertion electrode particle under potentiostatic and galvanostatic operation
  • Fracture Modeling of Lithium-Silicon Battery Based on Variable Elastic Moduli
  • Stress evolution with concentration-dependent compositional expansion in a silicon lithium-ion battery anode particle

Deep Reinforcement Learning for Autonomous Vehicles

Objective: To use RL framework for safe traversal of a self-driving car in CARLA simulator environment.

Github Repository

Reinforcement learning (RL) is an area of machine learning concerned with how intelligent agents ought to take actions in an environment in order to maximize the notion of cumulative reward. Reinforcement learning is one of three basic machine learning paradigms, alongside supervised learning and unsupervised learning. RL has very promising applications in various aspects of autonomous driving.

In this project, I used the Deep Q-Learning algorithm with Temporal difference learning update rule and dueling deep learning framework with value and advantage functions to compute the action values. I implemented a 3-layer CNN with the image from the onboard camera as the state input. The output of this layer is flattened and then divided into two outputs - a value function and an advantage function, which are then used to compute the action values. The action space is composed of discrete tuples of the throttle, brake, and steering values. The action values obtained from the neural network are mapped to discrete values, using the euclidean norm. These actions were then sent to the CARLA controller. Finally, the real-time reward is computed using the data from the wheel odometry, lane invasion, and collision sensors. Needless to say, I used separate training and target networks. For the experience relay, I used the Sum tree data structure for having a prioritized experience resampling.

In the intial stages of the simulation, the vehicle's steering response was unstable because of discrete values and low training. With time, this behaviour was minimised and the lateral stability was obtained. I ran the simulation for about 45 mins which covered almost 60% of the pre-planned trajectory. To understand the agent's performance on the vehicle control quantitatively, I used Stanley Steering control to compute the steering values at every time instant required to traverse the path.

References

  • Reinforcement Learning with Tensorflow by Sayon Dutta
  • CARMA: A Deep Reinforcement Learning Approach to Autonomous Driving by Matt Vitelli and Aran Nayebi
  • Behavioral Planning for Automated Vehicles using Deep Reinforcement Learning by Meha Kaushik
  • A Reinforcement Learning Based Approach for Automated Lane Change Maneuvers by Pin Wang, Ching-Yao Chan and Arnaud de La Fortelle

Traffic Sign Classification using CNN

Github Repository

The convolutional neural network (CNN) is a class of deep learning neural networks. CNNs represent a huge breakthrough in image recognition. They’re most commonly used to analyze visual imagery and are frequently working behind the scenes in image classification. They can be found at the core of everything from Facebook’s photo tagging to self-driving cars. Image classification is the process of taking an input (like a picture) and outputting a class (like “cat”) or a probability that the input is a particular class (“there’s a 90% probability that this input is a cat”).
A typical CNN consists of - Convolution layers, activation functions, pooling layers, fully connected layers.

In this project, I used a readily available dataset on Kaggle and deployed a Convolutional Neural Network model to act as a classifier for 43 different classes of traffic signs.
My neural network architecture: Input image -> ( Conv -> Max Pooling ) -> ( Conv -> Max Pooling ) -> ( Conv -> Max Pooling ) -> Dense -> Dense -> Dense Layer (softmax function).

After the first training and testing session, the testing accuracy wasn't satisfactory and the network seemed to overfit the given dataset. To rectify this, I changed the architecture by introducing a Dropout layer with 40% as chosen value and a fresh convolution + pooling pair. This led to a significant change in the testing accuracy and the problem of overfitting was rectified. I tested the framework over about 12,000 images and obtained an accuracy of 94%. For the complete project, Python programming language was used with the mentioned libraries - Numpy, Tensorflow, Keras, Matplotlib and OpenCV.

Summer Intern

KPIT Technologies Ltd, Pune, India

Certificate

My primary responsibility during the internship was to explore the APIs of AI compilers - TVM, Glow and MATLAB and leverage them to generate hardware-specific and optimized executable bundles which can be easily deployed on hardware backends. Deep learning models are significantly heavy when it comes to memory usage and time required to compute the results. This makes their deployment on edge devices, having lesser RAM and ROM, tedious. The existing DL frameworks namely, TensorFlow, PyTorch and ONNX provide support for training and deployment on CPUs and GPUs. However, when it comes to edge devices with low power capabilities, the optimization of these models becomes a challenging task.

In the first part of the internship, I leveraged the AI compilers to optimize well-known pretrained DL models in existing frameworks for LLVM and GPU devices. The customization options vary with respect to the compilers and each of them had different optimization schemes.
The models were selected or created so as to ensure that different types of architectures are considered. Then we decided the performance metrics which were then used to observe the relative performance of these compilers and frameworks. These metrics were essentially the representatives of the memory consumption and inference time of the selected models. Finally, we observed the relative behaviour of these compilers in terms of the performance metrics, their compatibility and efficiency to optimize the different model architectures and hardware-specific deployment capabilities they offered. The comparison performed was qualitative as well as quantitative so as to provide a better method of decision-making as per the user's application.
The codes were based on C++ and Python, varying slightly with respect to the compilers' and frameworks' API.

UG Research Assistant

Centre for Automotive Research, OSU

Certificate

The project dealt with conducting the performance evaluation of a medium fidelity Driver-in-the-Loop Simulator, present in the Driver Dynamics Lab at OSU. In the initial phase of the project, I reviewed the literature for Objective Motion Cueing Test (OMCT). It is applied by using a series of sinusoidal signals from 0.1 rad/s (0.0159 Hz) to 15.849 rad/s (2.515 Hz) - to each of the input channels of the Motion Cueing Algorithm (MCA), in order to measure the output amplitude and phase distortion of each of these channels. OMCT is a frequency-based method.

Based on the specifications of the simulator, we finalized the criteria and testing methodology using input signals generated using Simulink which are then applied to the platform using the simulator's interface. Finally, in order to capture and estimate the velocities and accelerations in 3D, we used linear variable differential transformers (LVDTs). The results of this experiment helped us in identifying the fidelity regions of the simulator with respect to its motion about different axes, using the modulus and phase plotted against the frequency of the signal. To get rid of the noise in sensor readings, we used the Butterworth filter.

In the next phase of the project, we looked into the transformation and filtering of the data collected from an inertial measurement unit (IMU) to be deployed on the simulator to capture its acceleration in different directions. The transformation was required so as to estimate the acceleration at a different point compared to the IMU's location. We collected the required data for 2 locations on the ego vehicle from CarSim for different maneuvers. In order to mimic the sensor's behaviour, we introduced random noise in the signals and deployed different filtering techniques to remove the same as much as possible. Using physics-based transformation for rigid body dynamics, we obtained the acceleration at the required coordinate in 3D and validated this against that obtained from CarSim. Finally, we implemented a Gaussian filter of configurable window length and the obtained errors were within acceptable limits.

The codes were based on MATLAB.

References

  • Evaluation of Motion Cueing Algorithms for a Limited Motion Platform Driver-in-Loop Simulator by Rubanraj Sekar
  • Objective Motion Cueing Test for Driving Simulators by M. Fischer et al.
  • On the Objective Evaluation of Motion Cueing in Vehicle Simulations by Sergio Casas-Yrurzum et al.
  • Relative motion of points on a body

Research Intern

ARMS Lab, IIT Bombay, India

Project title: Applications of Machine Learning Methods for intersection and lane-following for autonomous vehicles

Supervisors: Prof. Leena Vachhani | Prof. Arpita Sinha

Initially, I implemented a Finite State Machine consisting 4 states - Decelerate to stop, Stop, Follow lead vehicle, and Track speed with required transitions. This was done to understand the agent's behavior due to the brute force approach and hence can be used for comparing with results from the ML model OR this machine can be enhanced with Machine Learning tools. I was asked to define various situations including intersection handling and lane-following in the presence/absence of obstacles. This was tested in the CARLA simulator.

In the next phase of the project, I devised an automatic controller in Python that was used to roam around in the CARLA environment and collect datasets. These datasets comprised of images from the onboard camera sensor and real-time client measurements consisting of control commands, distance to nearby obstacles, etc. The Town-01 of the CARLA simulator was used. The ego vehicle as well as the other agents were modified to violate traffic light regulations. The controller incorporated collision avoidance which was activated/deactivated to collect a variety of data points favourable for the efficient training of the neural network.

Towards the end of the project, I had to define the complete problem using a Markov Decision Process representation with states, actions, rewards and subsequent states. The reward was evaluated as a discounted sum for a particular episode. But, these values might not be consistent due to variation in the reward accumulation and randomisation in the agent’s action selection behaviour. Hence, I had to normalize the rewards so as to avoid incorrect computations. The normalization was performed using the mean and standard deviation of the rewards.
